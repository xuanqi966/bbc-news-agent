Topic: UK government announces new housing target and planning reform

Raw material:

Prime Minister Keir Starmer announced on Tuesday a revised target to build 1.5 million new homes across England by the end of the current parliament in 2029, up from the previous target of 1.2 million.

Speaking at a press conference in Manchester, Starmer said: "We inherited a broken housing market, and tinkering at the edges will not fix it. This is about restoring the promise that hard work leads to a home of your own."

Housing Secretary Angela Rayner confirmed that mandatory housing targets for local councils will be reintroduced. Under the previous Conservative government, those targets were made advisory in December 2023.

The Office for Budget Responsibility estimated in March that meeting the 1.5 million target would require an average of 300,000 new homes per year — a rate not achieved in England since 1977.

Shadow Housing Secretary Kemi Badenoch criticised the plan, calling it "a press release pretending to be a policy" and arguing it would lead to overdevelopment of greenbelt land.

The government also published a review of the National Planning Policy Framework, which includes proposals to release "grey belt" land — previously developed or low-quality greenbelt — for housing. The consultation on the reforms opens on 4 March and runs for eight weeks.

A spokesperson for the Local Government Association welcomed additional funding but warned that councils were "fundamentally under-resourced" to process applications at the scale required.

The Home Builders Federation said its members had the capacity to build around 250,000 homes per year by 2027 if planning bottlenecks were removed, but a shortage of skilled trades would remain a limiting factor beyond that.
