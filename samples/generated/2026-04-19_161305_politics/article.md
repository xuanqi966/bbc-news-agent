# Government Reintroduces Mandatory Housing Targets to Tackle England's Shortage

The UK government has committed to building 1.5 million homes by 2029, reintroducing mandatory local targets to address a long-standing national housing crisis.

Prime Minister Keir Starmer announced the revised target, which represents a significant increase from the previous figure of 1.2 million new homes. The new objective aims to deliver 1.5 million units across England over the next five years.

"We inherited a broken housing market, and tinkering at the edges will not fix it. This is about restoring the promise that hard work leads to a home of your own," the Prime Minister said.

### What does this mean?

The central pillar of the reform is the return of mandatory housing targets for local councils. Housing Secretary Angela Rayner confirmed that these requirements will be reintroduced after they were made advisory in December 2023 under the previous Conservative government.

By making these targets compulsory again, the government aims to establish a consistent framework for delivery. However, the scale of the challenge is significant when viewed against historical benchmarks. To meet the 1.5 million goal, England must deliver 300,000 homes per year.

The Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) estimated in March that this annual rate is required to meet the government's target. However, a rate of 300,000 new homes per year has not been achieved in England since 1977. This nearly fifty-year gap highlights the systemic nature of the current housing shortage.

### The 'grey belt' strategy

To facilitate this growth, the government has published a review of the National Planning Policy Framework. A key proposal involves the strategic release of what ministers call "grey belt" land.

The government defines "grey belt" as land that is previously developed or consists of low-quality greenbelt. By identifying these specific areas for development, the strategy seeks to unlock land that was previously restricted under tighter planning conventions.

A formal consultation on these reforms is scheduled to open on 4 March. This process will run for eight weeks, allowing stakeholders to examine the proposed changes to the National Planning Policy Framework and the mechanisms for land release.

### Local and political friction

The plans have already met with criticism from the opposition. Shadow Housing Secretary Kemi Badenoch dismissed the announcement, describing it as "a press release pretending to be a policy."

Beyond the political debate in Westminster, local authorities have raised practical concerns regarding the implementation of these mandatory targets. The Local Government Association (LGA) warned that councils are currently "fundamentally under-resourced" to process the volume of applications required to meet the new targets.

The LGA’s position suggests that the administrative capacity of local planning departments remains a primary bottleneck. Without the necessary resources to manage the planning process, the transition from advisory to mandatory targets faces significant operational hurdles.

### Industry capacity

The private sector also faces constraints that could impact the government's timeline. The Home Builders Federation (HBF) stated that its members could potentially build 250,000 homes per year by 2027, provided that existing bottlenecks are removed.

However, the HBF identified a shortage of skilled trades as a major limiting factor for the industry. This labor shortage exists alongside the administrative challenges identified by local government.

As the consultation period approaches, the focus remains on whether these institutional shifts can bridge the gap between political targets and the practical reality of England’s housing supply. The government must now navigate the tension between its 1.5 million home ambition and the resource constraints cited by both councils and the construction industry.
