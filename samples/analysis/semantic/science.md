# Style notes: science

## Tone & voice
The tone is authoritative yet accessible, balancing scientific gravity with human interest. It avoids sensationalism in favor of measured, evidence-based reporting. Even when discussing dire climate data or political controversy, the voice remains neutral and observational, often employing a 'matter-of-fact' delivery for extraordinary statistics or historical events.

## Structure & framing
Articles typically lead with a significant milestone—a record broken, a death, or a birth—before expanding into broader scientific or historical context. There is a strong emphasis on 'then vs. now' comparisons, using historical benchmarks (e.g., 1961-1990 averages or 1944 weather reports) to frame current events. Complex topics like climate change are often broken down into digestible impacts on infrastructure and daily life.

## Vocabulary & register
The register is middle-ground: it utilizes precise scientific terminology (e.g., 'barometer', 'greenhouse gas emissions', 'liquefied natural gas') but clarifies it with 'plain English' descriptors like 'really hot days' or 'pleasant days'. Quantitative data is abundant, frequently citing percentages, temperatures, and specific dates to ground the narrative in hard evidence.

## Sourcing & attribution
Heavy reliance on institutional authority, specifically the Met Office, UN climate bodies, and academic experts. Attribution is explicit and frequent, often naming lead scientists or chief executives. For controversial topics, the BBC includes its own investigative transparency, citing 'leaked briefing documents' and 'highest editorial standards' alongside official denials.

## Distinctive markers
A recurring focus on record-breaking events ('first in thousands of years', 'second warmest year on record'). The articles frequently use 'bridge' sentences to connect scientific data to human consequences, such as how weather 'profoundly impact communities'. There is also a distinct habit of providing 'simple guides' or bulleted links to help the reader navigate complex global summits or climate concepts.

## Example phrases
- "significantly more really hot days"
- "human-induced climate change"
- "the figures were the same both times"
- "living beyond the years of a wild croc"
- "a dangerous upheaval for our ecosystems"
- "rigorously researched according to highest editorial standards"
