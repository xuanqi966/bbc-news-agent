# Style notes: health

## Tone & voice
The tone is empathetic yet clinical, balancing human-interest narratives with objective medical data. It often adopts a cautionary stance when discussing health trends, while maintaining a supportive voice for patients navigating systemic failures or chronic conditions.

## Structure & framing
Articles typically lead with a personal anecdote or a specific case study to humanize the medical issue, followed by a transition into broader policy, statistics, or official guidelines. They frequently use 'problem-solution' framing, highlighting a healthcare gap (e.g., dental shortages, abortion access) and then presenting the government or institutional response.

## Vocabulary & register
The register is accessible middle-ground journalism. It avoids dense medical jargon where possible, or defines it immediately (e.g., explaining BMI or GLP-1). It uses evocative but grounded descriptors for physical and mental states, such as 'trauma,' 'constant pain,' and 'body dysmorphia.'

## Sourcing & attribution
Sourcing is a blend of 'lived experience' (first-name patients like Katie or Lyn), professional bodies (British Dental Association, NICE), and academic experts (dermatologists, dietitians). Official government responses are almost always included as a concluding or balancing counterpoint.

## Distinctive markers
The use of bulleted lists for medical checklists or survey data is common. Articles often include 'service-journalism' elements, such as explaining how to spot symptoms (the ABCDE mole checklist) or providing context on how to access specific NHS treatments.

## Example phrases
- "living in pain, unable to speak or eat properly"
- "the gaunt 'Ozempic face'"
- "evidence-based support for reproductive choices"
- "a deepening crisis"
- "the difference between life and death"
- "a dysfunctional contract"
