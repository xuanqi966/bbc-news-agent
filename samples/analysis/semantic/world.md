# Style notes: world

## Tone & voice
The tone is measured, observant, and detached, maintaining a neutral 'view from nowhere' even when reporting on volatile political events. It balances gravity with a touch of narrative flair, often starting articles with a thematic hook or a cultural reference to humanize complex policy or legal issues.

## Structure & framing
Articles typically follow a 'micro-to-macro' structure: opening with a specific anecdote or individual experience before pivoting to broader geopolitical or economic implications. They frequently use 'thematic signposting'—short, punchy paragraphs that summarize the stakes before diving into chronological or evidentiary detail.

## Vocabulary & register
The register is high-level but accessible, favoring precise verbs and descriptive nouns over emotive adjectives. It employs a mix of formal diplomatic terminology and vivid, plain-English metaphors to explain systemic friction.

## Sourcing & attribution
Sourcing is multi-layered, blending official government statements and legal filings with direct testimony from 'on-the-ground' subjects. Attribution is meticulous, often specifying the medium of the quote (e.g., 'said on X', 'in recorded messages', or 'through tears') to provide context for the source's emotional state or platform.

## Distinctive markers
The use of 'contextual callbacks'—briefly reminding the reader of a subject's past controversies or historical significance—is a staple. Articles also frequently include 'bulleted sidebars' or 'related link' headers integrated into the body text to provide a broader web of coverage.

## Example phrases
- "probably wasn’t thinking about the economic boost"
- "marks a sharp departure from India's trepidation"
- "like a shovel trying to empty the ocean"
- "became embroiled in a spat"
- "insufficient admissible evidence to file criminal charges"
- "taking the leap into 24-hour nightlife"
